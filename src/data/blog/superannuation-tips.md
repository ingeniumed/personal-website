## Background

Unlike Canada, Australia doesn't have a TFSA, RRSP or even a LIRA. Instead, it has the superannuation with contributions into it being mandatory by every employer/employee and its balance untouchable (barring certain circumstances) until preservation age is reached. Due to most people just leaving this on autopilot, it's easy to leave extra money on the table.

So I wrote this post to collect together some of the optimizations to help enhance the balance at retirement, as well as touch on investing outside one's super.

## Investment Strategy and Fees

It's easy to just select the default investment strategy that your super platform picks for you.

On Australian Retirement Trust that'd be [Lifecycle Investment Strategy](https://www.australianretirementtrust.com.au/investments/lifecycle-investment-strategy). It has 3 buckets within it - High Growth, Balanced and Cash Pool where the money goes based on your age. The first two buckets have their fees set to 0.70% and 0.65%, respectively. This is on top of ART's [standard admin fees](https://www.australianretirementtrust.com.au/investments/fees). On a balance of 100,000 that's about $700 annually being charged for the high growth option or $650 for the balanced option. That's a lot of money going towards this default option especially given the returns for these options isn't that great compared to what one is paying in fees.

On the other hand, each of these superfunds including ART offer index investment options or even access to ETFs directly. Those options have much lower fees compared to their actively managed options, and would have better returns as well.

So if we take the case of our $100,000 and instead invested it in 20% [australian shares](https://www.australianretirementtrust.com.au/investments/australian-shares-index) and 80% [international shares](https://www.australianretirementtrust.com.au/investments/international-shares-unhedged-index) our fees would be around 0.08% which is $96 and our returns would be a lot higher in the long run as well.

Conclusion: Review your investment strategy within your super, and consider switching to the share options that mirrors an index.

## Concessional Contributions

Always always see if you can salary sacrifice extra into your super on top of the regular contributions that you are making. This is especially handy if you have a concessional balance left from previous years as that gets carried forward for 5 years. Also, by salary sacrificing you are reducing your taxable income and thereby the tax that you pay.

Keep track of how close you are to the balance as you only get the 15% tax rate under it.

I highly recommend using [this calculator](https://paycalculator.com.au) to see how much you can salary sacrifice safely, as well as your ATO account to see what your prior concessional balance is.

## Switching Funds

If you aren't happy with your superfund due to the fees or the investment options not being good enough switch away. You don't need to stick with the one you signed up with.

Also, ensure you collate all your funds into one super account.

## Investing outside Super

Depending on your retirement goals, it'd be handy to think about investing outside your superfund as well. This could be if you want your balance accessible before preservation age, or are interested in FIRE.

### Stake and ETFs

The easiest way to get started is to sign up for an investment platform like [Stake](https://hellostake.com/r/smithak436?name=Smitha) and buy a simple all in one ETF like [DHHF](https://www.betashares.com.au/fund/diversified-all-growth-etf/). I call this the DHHF and chill approach. I personally prefer my investment platform to use [CHESS](https://www.commsec.com.au/support/help-centre/About-the-sharemarket/What-is-CHESS.html) as that makes me personally feel secure, but platforms using the custodian model will have cheaper fees at the risk of not owning the underlying holdings yourself.

A simple all in one ETF has the advantage of not needing to do any balancing work or any extra math. You just keep buying this one ETF and let it do all the work. It's a high growth ETF that takes care of hitting the right geographic split, sector split and has a decent MER as well. It's also fine to do the whole split yourself, but the important part is to target high growth using all equities/no bonds if you are not anywhere close to retirement.

### Offset

If you have a homeloan, I highly recommend taking advantage of the offset account. Anything in this account lowers your mortgage by said amount for interest calculation purposes. So if you are fully offset, your entire mortgage payment goes towards the principal and you simply pay it down much faster.

Depending on the interest rate you are paying, and the tax bracket you are in it'll be tax advantageous to target the offset account rather than invest outside the super. It's also fine to balance both of these.
